Metaphysics of the non-physical

I'm almost certain that my interest in academic philosophy began with the mind-body problem, so it is fitting that my first piece of writing for Atlas, Rise be on this subject. The idea for this blog post first came in summer of 2023, when I was living in Boston. I was at a Dojo house barbeque, and as I recall, a debate about the singular importance of human conciousness in the universe (which I believe) took a turn for philosophical when we began discussing the physical/non-physical nature of the mind. During this conversation, I made certain arguments similar, though less refined, to some of what follows. All of this is to say that I would like to thank the barbeque-goers who, as a result of our idea-exchange, indirectly helped me write this piece!

To offer a succinct exposition to the unacquainted reader, the mind-body problem can be a posited as a "mutual inconsistency" between 4 metaphysical claims (this treatment is due to [1], an excellent book that I read during my first year of university for an introductory philosophy class):

  1. The mind is non-physical
  2. The body is physical
  3. The mind and the body interact
  4. Non-physical things cannot interact with physical things
Clearly we cannot accept all of these premises simultaneously without arriving at a contradiction, despite each seeming at least somewhat plausible. We are therefore tasked with resolving this inconsistency. Some of the great thinkers of human history have attempted solutions which, more or less, amount to simply rejecting one of these claims wholesale. One of the most notable radical examples of this is Leibniz's psychophysical parallelism via "pre-established harmony", which asserts that the mind and the body do not interact. Instead, every distinct substance interacts only with itself, but by the guiding hand of some divine power (God), there is a universal harmony, a pre-programmed synchronization between the independent actions [2]. Perhaps I will write more about my thoughts on parallelism in a future essay, but for the time being, let me simply state that I respect the boldness of those who show no mercy, and are willing to cleave from the inconsistent list. There is a certain satisfaction in modes of thought which "go all the way". But, much to the chagrin of my desire for no-half-measures, I, like many contemporary thinkers who ponder the same problem, believe that a softer touch is needed in order to make progress towards resolution.

As the title suggests, I would like to sketch a preliminary metaphysics for the "non-physical" world. In my opinion, this first requires a discussion the world vs. the parts of the world that the mind can interact with. First of all, I am categorically not a relativist: the existence of an objective world is something that I completely believe, and will accept throughout this essay (more on this another time). However, I also believe that the aspects of the world that are accessible to reason and conceptualization in the mind form a proper subset of all phenomena. In other words,

Claim 1. The mind is finite within the objective world. In other words, there exist phenomena in the objective world with which the mind cannot interact via conceptualization.

As a "non-example", consider the UV spectrum of light, much of which lies outside the grasp of our human senses. It would be highly inaccurate to classify UV light as a phenomenon lying outside of "the accessible", because of the fact that we can reason about the nature of UV light: we can model its behaviour and interactions via physics, and observe high predictive-power of our models. The sum total of this understanding forms our conceptualization of UV light, and so it is accessible. We must also be careful to distinguish between what the mind cannot interact with, and what the mind has not yet interacted with. Imagine for a moment that this piece was written at the end of the 19th century instead of 2024. Albert Einstein is still a young child, and has not yet had even a modicum of insight about relativity or the geometric nature of gravity. Does this mean that the phenomena of "time dialation" or "curved spacetime" lie outside of the accessible? It does not: clearly we can conceptualize these ideas, but when Einstein was a boy, we simply had not yet done so. Applying this same logic forward in time, I would say it is a safe bet that there is a enormity of physics that we do not yet understand or access, but could access eventually. All such physics lies within the set of accessible things.
